Being listed as “missing” almost never means the worst

Communications across much of the country are down. Tens of thousands of “missing” reports very likely include people who are simply out of contact —no signal— and many reports are duplicates. Not being able to reach someone today doesn't mean the worst has happened to them.

Rescue teams treat the first 48 to 72 hours as the most critical, but that window extends when people have access to air and water. It's a real reason for hope, not a countdown clock.

Before you search, breathe. Here's how to do it well.

These tools are free and never ask for payment. If someone demands money for information about a missing person, it's a scam: walk away and report it.

Citizen platforms

Platforms built by people

Fast, free and widely used by families inside Venezuela — but community-run and unverified. Use them knowing exactly what they are.

Verified — official channel (Red Cross / ICRC): confidential and free

Venezuela Te Busca

Citizen · unverified

The most transparent of the citizen platforms. It has a public creator with a full name —digital entrepreneur Julia Alessandra Mariano (@juliaamariano), who has spoken to the press— and it's honest about its limits: it openly says it doesn't verify what's posted and that the data is the responsibility of whoever submits it. It lets you register a case, search reports, and mark someone as “found,” with filters by name, age and location.

Quick guide

How to search safely

Based on the platforms' own recommendations. Small habits that save time and prevent mistakes.

  • Search before you report. Check whether a case already exists so you don't duplicate it.
  • Give details that help. Full name, age, distinctive features, the clothes they were wearing and, above all, the last known location. A recent photo makes a difference.
  • Leave a reliable contact. It's what allows verification and lets them notify you if there's news.
  • Report on more than one platform, but note where. So you can update them all.
  • Let people know the moment you find them. Marking someone as “found” frees up rescuers and volunteers.
  • Be careful with viral figures. The numbers change every hour; always cite a date and time, or go to the live source.
  • Take care of yourself too. Lean on family, neighbors or psychological support lines. The waiting wears you down.
  • Watch out for scams. No one legitimate will charge you for information about a missing person.
Also, informally

Part of the search has moved to Instagram and X. Alianza por Venezuela, a migrant-led initiative in Argentina, helps Venezuelans abroad reconnect with their families. It's an informal channel: useful for spreading the word, but without the verification of the official channels above. Search for its name on social media and confirm who's behind it before sharing personal data.
